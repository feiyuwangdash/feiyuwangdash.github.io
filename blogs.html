Why the Fastest Individuals Make the Slowest Team: Selection-on-Noise in the Hakone Ekiden Union Team

2026-05-14

Every January, the Hakone Ekiden fields a 21st “all-star” team composed of the ten fastest individual finishers at the prior qualifier whose own universities failed to qualify. On paper a top-tier team; in practice, famously slow. Using 11 editions (2015–2026), per-leg results for 1,600+ runner-legs, and personal-best half-marathon times, I rule out the fan “mentality” story via within-runner identification (the same runner runs about the same pace whether on the union team or their own college team), then walk through three guesses for what does explain the ~5-minute gap. Sandbagging is ruled out empirically; lucky-day selection (regression to the mean from picking on a single noisy race) accounts for roughly half; and the rest is a persistent runner-level gap whose exact channel the data can’t isolate — plausible candidates are specialized Hakone training, leg-matching by a familiar coach, and course experience. A worked example of how selection on a noisy single measurement manufactures what looks like a behavioral pattern.

Do UEFA Referees Favor Players From Their Own Country? An Econometric Look at Five Seasons of Champions League and Europa League

2026-05-07

The 2026 Bayern–PSG semifinal raised an old fan suspicion: does a referee call fewer fouls on his countrymen? Using FBref data on 1,531 UCL/UEL matches from 2021–22 to 2025–26, the descriptive comparison shows a 20% gap in fouls per 90 between same-country and other player-matches — but a two-way fixed-effect specification (player + match FE) erases it entirely. A team-level win-rate effect appears, then collapses once a club-quality control is added. A worked example of how a 20% raw gap can dissolve once you account for whom you’re actually comparing.
